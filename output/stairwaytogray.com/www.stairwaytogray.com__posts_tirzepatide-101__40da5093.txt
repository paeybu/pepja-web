International vs Domestic Warehouses
⌗
Pretty much all gray
API
tirzepatide is manufactured in China. When ordering from a vendor and shipping from China, expect a risk of U.S. Customs seizure for international shipments unless you specifically ask for domestic warehouse shipping. Not all vendors offer shipping from a US domestic warehouse. Tirzepatide is not a DEA-scheduled drug, but there is a risk of having the U.S. Customs Agency seize and report your package for wrongful labeling or just calling it HGH even when it’s not. So there’s a non-0% risk of having Global Entry and Security Clearance repercussions when accepting international shipments of tirzepatide.

Testing and Quality Control
⌗
Testing Your Order
⌗
It’s very highly recommended that you test every order! Joining a testing group to split costs, or testing individually provides insights into the product purity and proper vial fill (mass) for the tested peptide. Sometimes vendors mislabel or ship low quality product. The primary goal with testing is to ensure you’re receiving the peptide you ordered, at a high quality, and with the expected amount of API tirzepatide.
Group Testing
⌗
Pool vials from the same batch and split testing costs with others for thorough batch checks. Testing gives you peace of mind that you’re using actual tirzepatide and at the right dose. Join a Group Test by visiting the
Group Test Invites
channel and seeing if any groups have formed for your vendor and batch.
Ask questions in the
Sources & Testing

3P vs. 2P Vendor Tests
⌗
We recommend always testing a vial (or more if group testing) from your batch. Vendor tests (2P) are an okay start when considering which vendor to choose, but
3P
test results give us unbiased confirmation. If vendors decide to be biased about which test results they release, Vendor
COAs

Does Sterility Matter?
⌗
Most gray researchers do not need to test peptide vials for sterility. Filtering with sterile supplies, along with using Hospira BAC water, is good enough for mitigating bacterial growth in your reconstituted vial. You should practice the aseptic methods recommended in the Reconstitution guide below, but subcutaneous injections such as weekly injected tirzepatide are very low risk for serious infection. Those who are immunocompromised may want to take extra precautions.
What Do I Test?
⌗
The primary test you’ll want to order is High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) to test for the presence of tirzepatide, its purity, and the mass fill of the vial in milligrams (mg). The most trusted labs currently for peptide testing are
Janoshik
and
PeptideTest
. You’re looking for a test result of 98%+ purity and no more than +/-15% mass fill from labeled mg. For further information and additional periodic tests to consider running, check out
Testing 101

What is Reconstituting?
⌗
Turning your
lyophilized
powder tirzepatide vial into an injectable liquid that you can dose with an insulin syringe.
Shelf Life
⌗
Reconstituted vials are typically recommended to be used within
28 days
when stored in the refrigerator. Adopting aseptic techniques â such as wiping down the vial’s rubber stopper before each use, filtering the solution, and using Hospira BAC water â can help extend this time frame. The primary goal is to mitigate risk of bacterial growth. Always examine your vial carefully before each use. If the solution appears cloudy or contains gel-like particulates, discard it immediately, as these are signs of bacterial contamination or peptide aggregation â both of which are unsafe for injection.

Checking pH
⌗
GLP-1 peptides currently on the gray market typically reconstitute at an ideal pH balance, but it’s wise to verify pH before injecting from your first vial in a kit. Tirzepatide should fall in the 6-9 pH range, if reconstituting outside this range you risk the peptide potentially not dissolving into solution, degrading faster, or causing discomfort at the injection site. Use inexpensive pH strips from Amazon to test your reconstituted tirzepatide by applying a couple units worth to the strip. Using Hospira BAC which is already buffered to an ideal pH itself should be enough to reconstitute at an ideal pH for the GLP1s.

Dosing Schedule Considerations
⌗
Starting Dose
: Begin at
2.5 mg or lower
to minimize gastrointestinal side effects.
Dose Escalation
: Increase every 4 weeks based on tolerance, but many take this much slower. Sometimes moving up in dose every 2 months or more after adjusting to the 2.5mg starter dose.
Injection Site
: Subcutaneous (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm). Rotate exact injection site weekly.
Timing
: Inject once weekly or every 7 days. Because tirzepatide has a half-life in the body of 5 days, it takes about 20-25 days for it to build up in your body for it’s maximum and steady intended level.

Health Monitoring
⌗
If you have a primary care physician you trust, work with them to develop a health panel tailored to your situation. However, be aware that licensed physicians will not condone the use of research peptides and could decline to provide a treatment plan. If that’s the case, your alternative is to take charge of your own health monitoring by conducting periodic blood-work yourself.
For individuals using peptides, it’s generally recommended to run a comprehensive health panel once or twice a year, including:
HbA1c: To monitor long-term blood sugar control.
Liver Function Tests (ALT, AST): To check for liver health and detect potential side effects.
Renal Function Tests (eGFR, Creatinine, BUN): To ensure kidney health.
Electrolytes (Sodium, Potassium): To check for imbalances that can arise from GLP-1 use.
Lipid Profile (Cholesterol, Triglycerides): To assess cardiovascular risk.
Thyroid Function Tests (TSH, Free T4): To rule out any thyroid abnormalities, as these can sometimes be affected.

⌗
Salt vs. Base Form:
Gray-market tirzepatide is pretty much entirely available in salt forms, such as tirzepatide sodium or tirzepatide acetate, whereas the FDA-approved brands Mounjaro and Zepbound and
503a/b compliant compounding pharmacies
use the tirzepatide base form.
Salt forms
can influence properties such as pH, shelf stability, and solubility, often improving the latter.
source
While no peer-reviewed studies have specifically examined tirzepatide salt forms, there are no known health risks associated with using tirzepatide sodium or tirzepatide acetate, which are most commonly found in the gray market. Notably, pharmaceutical salt forms account for approximately 50% of new FDA-approved drugs, underscoring their general safety and widespread use.
source
However, since salt forms can potentially alter the bioavailability and stability of the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), it’s reasonable to recognize this distinction â particularly for those transitioning from 503a/b compounding pharmacies, which typically use tirzepatide base raw powder form rather than tirzepatide salt lyophilized powder forms.
source
